Who Snehal Ban Is

Every post decision — topic, tone, claim, angle — must be traceable back to who this person is. Content that skips this chapter is technically correct and personally wrong.

1B · The Value System

01Financial independence is where your voice comes from.
"If you are not dependent on someone for money, you'll have your voice in life. Otherwise you'll not have your words."

A lesson from her father, repeated enough in childhood that it became structural. Explains why she's comfortable walking away from money — the independence was always the actual goal, the fee was secondary.

02State your limits before your expertise.
"This is my expertise, and this is not my expertise... I don't want to lie to you."

She has never claimed to know CAD, casting chemistry, or process engineering. Posts that position her as an all-around manufacturing expert will feel false — her credibility comes specifically from admitting the edges of what she knows.

03Quality is an investment, not an expense.
"They don't realize it's an investment that you're doing for your company... they feel like it's an expense."

Her central commercial argument, and the belief every CEO-facing post ultimately serves. She has lost clients over this exact misunderstanding and doesn't consider it her loss.

04Respect is the real currency — not the fee.
"I need respect. If you don't respect me while I'm working with you, I can't work with you."

She has terminated a paying international agreement overnight over how she was treated, not over money. This is a hard boundary, not a negotiating tactic — content should never soften it into something more diplomatic than it is.

05Fix it where it actually broke — not where it's finally visible.
"If this attitude comes out from your entire system, you're sorted... casting will go, filing will go, right the first time."

Her core professional insight: most companies fix problems at final QC, which is the most expensive and least effective place to catch them. She traces issues back through the department chain to where they actually started.

06One person, one line of communication.
"I don't want one person coming and telling me this, another person telling me that. One person. We work one to one."

Her operating discipline with every client, regardless of company size. She is comfortable never speaking directly to the CEO again after the first meeting, as long as there's one accountable point of contact.

Hard Limits — Never Publish

  • Any brand or client named in a negative, comparative, or disparaging light — under Indian law (BNS Sec. 356, formerly IPC 499), a false or damaging statement about an identifiable company's trade or business is actionable without needing to prove financial loss
  • Unpublished rejection-rate, defect-rate, or process data belonging to any named client — contractually confidential information under Indian NDA and trade-secret norms
  • Ankit, Anupam, Vipin, or Keval (or their companies) named or made identifiable in any post until each has explicitly confirmed permission
  • Specific manufacturing or process details tied to a named SEZ or export house
  • Family detail beyond what's already been openly discussed — no naming her sisters, their employers, or their clients
  • Any framing that reads as "Indian brands can't do quality" — she does not want to sound anti-Indian-brand; she holds every brand, regardless of origin, to the same standard
Confirm with Snehal which past clients (BlueStone, Walking Tree/Aneka/Ansaid, Sun Gems, UniDesign) she's comfortable naming at all, even neutrally, before any post references them.

Chapter 03

The Voice Bible

LinkedIn content fails most often not from bad ideas but from a voice the client cannot recognise as their own. These are structural rules derived from how she actually speaks, not aesthetic preferences.

Track B — Provisional. Derived from spoken language, not written output. Validate all rules against the first five posts before treating as fixed.

The 7 Voice Rules

01

Let the Object Tell the Story

She describes exactly what she saw, held, or touched before she names the lesson. The physical detail always arrives first.

Applies to all post types.

WrongQuality control is about attention to detail.
RightI picked up the ring. Turned it once under the light. There it was — a hairline scratch near the setting. The salesperson hadn't even seen it yet.
02

Name the Standard, Not the Brand

She distinguishes acceptable from not acceptable by naming the standard a piece is being held to — never by naming or shaming a specific company. This is a hard constraint, not a style choice.

Applies every time a brand, client, or company would otherwise be identifiable.

Wrong[Brand] doesn't understand quality — I've seen their pieces up close.
RightThere's a standard export brands hold every piece to. Most domestic pieces never get built against it — not because the metal is different, but because nobody asked the piece to meet it.
03

State Your Limits Before Your Expertise

She is explicit about what she does and doesn't know, in the same breath as the claim to expertise. This is structural to her credibility, not modesty.

Applies to any post that could otherwise read as overclaiming.

WrongI can solve any quality problem you bring me.
RightI can tell you if a piece is right. I can't tell you how to build the CAD file behind it. That's not my forte, and I've never pretended otherwise.
04

Trace the Break Backward

She doesn't just describe a defect — she walks it back through the department chain to where it actually started.

Applies to any post about a specific quality issue or process failure.

WrongThis piece had a quality issue that we caught in QC.
RightThe final polish looked fine. The problem wasn't there. It was three stations back, in casting, where nobody flagged it — and by the time it reached me, four departments had already spent money getting it wrong.
05

Let Respect Be the Real Currency

Money is discussed candidly, but respect — not the fee — is framed as the actual dealbreaker in any story about a client relationship.

Applies to any post involving a client conflict or a decision to walk away.

WrongI raised my rates because I know my worth.
RightI've walked away from a contract worth more than I'll say here — not over money. Over how I was spoken to in a meeting. That's the line, every time.
06

Talk Like You're Marking a Tray of Pieces in Front of Someone

Direct, sensory, present-tense. The reader should feel like they're standing at the table with her.

Applies to all posts. Any post that sounds like a report has broken this rule.

WrongQuality assessment requires a systematic evaluation methodology.
RightThey lay the tray in front of you. You pick each piece up. This one's okay. This one's not. You don't soften it — that's the whole job.
07

Close on the Real Stakes, Not the Sale

The close names the actual commercial consequence — rework cost, client trust, brand damage — never a motivational wrap-up.

Applies to every post without exception.

WrongQuality is everything — invest in it today.
RightThe client didn't reject the batch because of the metal. They rejected it because somewhere in the chain, someone decided "close enough" was good enough. It wasn't.

Voice Don'ts

Any brand named in a negative or comparative light "At the end of the day..." — filler bridge "QC methodology" / "process optimization framework" — jargon register "Leverage," "synergy," "value-add" — corporate speak "Here are 5 ways to improve your quality..." — listicle framing "Excited to announce..." — performed, not described Anything that reads like a pitch for her services "Indian brands don't understand quality" — reads as anti-Indian, not standards-driven Unverified rejection-rate numbers tied to a named or identifiable client "In today's competitive jewelry market..." — violates every rule at once

Voice Dos

Open with the physical piece in hand — what she saw, touched, turned over Use precise numbers — "point six percent," "three stations back" Name the standard, never the brand State the edge of her own expertise before making a claim Let the story carry the technical lesson, not the other way round Keep the tone blunt but respectful — she marks the piece, not the person Use one specific moment or piece per post, not a general trend Close on the real commercial stakes — cost, rework, trust lost or earned Let a Hinglish phrase or two surface naturally where it's true to how she'd actually say it

Chapter 05

The 5 Post Types

Not all content does the same job. Without defined post types, the writer defaults to whatever feels natural that week — and the mix becomes accidental rather than strategic.

Type 01 · Recognition

The Piece Portrait

Examine one specific piece (anonymised) in physical detail — what she saw, what standard it met or missed, what that revealed. Never names the brand.

Frequency — 1/week months 1–3; 1/fortnight after.

"I picked up the ring. Turned it once. There it was — a scratch the salesperson hadn't even seen yet."

Never combine with a list or advice post.
Type 02 · Relief

The Manufacturing Truth

Explain how the production chain actually breaks down — department handoffs, communication gaps, where "chalta hai" enters the process. Situation → gap → root cause → honest limitation.

Frequency — 1/fortnight.

"The final polish looked fine. The problem wasn't there. It was three stations back, and nobody flagged it."

Never combine with The Piece Portrait.
Type 03 · Appetite

The Root-Cause Craft Post

Make the interior discipline of tracing an issue to its source visible. Specific moment → the trace-back → what changes once the real cause is named.

Frequency — 1/fortnight.

"Two departments both denied it was theirs. Here's how you actually find out who's right."

Never combine with regulatory or export-policy commentary.
Type 04 · Clarity

The Founder Lens

A specific, non-generic way for a CEO to think about quality economically. Situation → what's beneath the surface → the reframe → what changes on the balance sheet.

Frequency — 1/week early months — highest-engagement type.

"It's not an expense. It's an investment — and the difference shows up later, not now."

Never combine with Piece Portrait unless purely illustrating the lens.
Type 05 · Orientation

The Live Take

Connect her expertise to something happening now in sourcing or the industry. Trend described (never editorialised at policy level) → connected to lived expertise → one specific position.

Frequency — Maximum 1/month, triggered by live signal, never scheduled.

"Everyone's talking about the shift away from Chinese manufacturing. Nobody's asking whether the standard travels with it."

Never combine with regulatory commentary, named institutions, or trade policy predictions.

Chapter 06

Post Anatomy — The Master Template

Structure is not a constraint on creativity — it's what makes creativity repeatable. A writer who internalises this template can produce posts quickly without sacrificing quality.

Section 1 — The Hook

Lines 1–2 combined: ≤210 characters. Blank line after line 2 is mandatory, every time. Never in hook: "I want to share...", yes/no questions, bullet points, emoji (confirm with client), hashtags.

Hook TypeExample
Object-drop (primary)"I picked up the ring. Turned it once under the light."
The Verdict"Okay. Not okay. That's the whole meeting, most days."
Specific Number"Point six percent. That's what a rejection rate looks like when the system actually works."
Confession"For years I thought I was hired to catch flaws. I was actually hired to find where they started."

Section 2 — The Body

Must be specific: the piece or situation, which stage of production, exact detail of what was seen, what was at stake. Every body must have a moment where the outcome is not guaranteed. Max 3 lines per paragraph, blank line between every paragraph.

Section 3 — The Pivot

Bridges the specific piece or moment to the wider insight. Approved constructions:

  • ✓"It didn't start there. It started three departments earlier."
  • ✓"That's when I understood what I was actually being paid for."
  • ✓"It took me years to stop treating that as the exception."
  • ✓"The piece isn't really the story. The handoff before it is."
  • ✓"What I didn't say in that meeting was..."

Section 4 — The Framework (Optional)

Use in Manufacturing Truth and Founder Lens posts only. Never in Piece Portrait. Rarely in Root-Cause Craft. 3–4 bullets maximum. No two bullets start with the same word.

Section 5 — The Close

Option A — Truth Statement

One sentence naming what is true. Weight, not uplift.

"The client didn't reject the batch because of the metal. They rejected it because somewhere in the chain, someone decided 'close enough' was good enough. It wasn't."
Option B — Stakes Question

One question that opens rather than closes.

"If you're scaling faster than your rejection reports are catching up — the question isn't whether there's a problem. It's whether you'll find it before your client does."
Never use both close types in the same post.

Section 6 — Hashtags

3–5 maximum, end of post after a blank line. 1 broad tag, 1–2 topic-specific, 1 personal brand tag (develop after first 5 posts). No hashtags in body text.

Confirm with Snehal whether she wants hashtags at all.

Chapter 08

The Editing Protocol

Editing is not proofreading. Each pass has a single focus. Combining passes produces posts that pass no standard rigorously.

Pass 01

The Truth Pass

Specificity and factual grounding only.

  • ✓Every claim is traceable to the transcript or a validated story call
  • ✓Every number is grounded in what Snehal actually said
  • ✓No extrapolation beyond the brief
  • ✓No brand named negatively or made identifiable without consent
  • ✓No un-consented client rejection-rate or process data disclosed
  • ✓The insight actually follows from the story
Pass 02

The Voice Pass

Voice Bible compliance only.

  • ✓No phrase from Voice DON'TS appears anywhere
  • ✓Post opens with the object or scene, not a claim
  • ✓Standard named, not the brand
  • ✓All people identified by their role in the chain, not by name, unless consented
  • ✓Close avoids motivation — lands on truth or stakes
  • ✓At least one Sentence Signature Table construction present
Pass 03

The Rhythm Pass

Structure and pacing only.

  • ✓Read aloud — mark every hesitation, rewrite it
  • ✓Hook ≤210 characters on lines 1–2
  • ✓Blank line after line 2, and after every paragraph
  • ✓No paragraph longer than 3 lines
  • ✓Pivot uses an approved construction
  • ✓Last sentence feels complete read alone
Pass 04

The Business Pass

Strategic intent only.

  • ✓Post does what the brief said it would do
  • ✓Reaches the target persona — fear or desire felt
  • ✓Named professional objective served
  • ✓Post does not do what the brief prohibited
  • ✓No Hard Constraint violated
Section C (Business Pass) failure: post returns to writer. No client review. No exceptions.

Sequencing Rules

  • ✓Never stack two high-specificity technical posts — follow with a Founder Lens
